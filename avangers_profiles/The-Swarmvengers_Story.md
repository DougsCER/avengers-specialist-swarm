# The Swarmvengers: When the World Sent an RFP

It arrived at dawn, on every screen on Earth at once — phones, billboards, smartwatches, a toaster in Ohio. A single document, glowing gold, beamed down from a fleet of ships parked politely in orbit:

**REQUEST FOR PLANET. Deadline: nightfall. One submission per species. No do-overs.**

The Vorlani Collective, it turned out, didn't conquer worlds. They *procured* them. And humanity had been invited to bid for its own survival. Submit a flawless proposal by sunset, and Earth stays in business. Miss a clause, fumble a number, sound like everyone else — and the lights go out. Forever.

The world panicked. Governments formed committees. Committees formed sub-committees. The toaster kept displaying the deadline.

And in a quiet office that smelled of cold coffee and destiny, one figure stayed calm. The **Coordinator**. She didn't write the proposal herself — that was never the point. She knew the truth every great leader knows: *you don't beat an impossible deadline alone. You beat it with a swarm.*

She made four calls.

---

**Pricing** arrived first, sleeves already rolled up, a calculator strapped to each wrist like twin pistols. She could look at *anything* — a spaceship, a sandwich, the survival of eight billion people — and instantly see its true cost, no padding, no panic. "They expect us to overbid out of fear," she said, eyes narrowing at the alien terms. "So we won't. I'll find the number that's honest *and* irresistible." Her fingers blurred. Decimal points fell into place like soldiers.

**Legal** came next, calm as a closed door, carrying a shield made entirely of fine print. While the others read the offer for what it said, he read it for what it *hid* — and the Vorlani had hidden plenty. Sub-clause 12, paragraph 9: a loophole that would've quietly signed away the Moon. "Caught it," he murmured, planting his shield over the trap. "They cannot beat us on a technicality. Not on my watch."

**Technical Fit** practically dove headfirst into the spec, surfacing covered in diagrams. His gift was alignment — making sure humanity's grand promise actually *matched* what the world needed, and not just what looked dazzling on a slide. "Anyone can promise the stars," he said, sketching furiously. "I make sure the bolts actually fit the holes. Our solution has to *work*, not just sparkle."

And **Competitive** slipped in last, having already scanned the dark between the stars. Because of course Earth wasn't the only bidder. "Forty-three other planets are answering this same RFP," she warned, spreading their pitches across the table like a poker hand. "Most are louder than us. Richer than us. But none of them know what makes *us* the obvious choice — and I do."

---

Here was the magic, the thing the Vorlani never expected: the Swarmvengers didn't work in a line, one after another, politely waiting their turn. They worked **all at once** — four brilliant threads blazing in parallel, Pricing crunching while Legal shielded while Technical built while Competitive maneuvered. The Coordinator stood at the center of the storm, catching every output midair and weaving it into a single, seamless answer. To anyone watching, it looked less like an office and more like a constellation lighting up.

The sun sank lower. The Vorlani fleet hummed. Across the planet, people who had never prayed before started bargaining with the toaster.

By dusk, the proposal was magnificent. Priced fair. Legally bulletproof. Technically sound. Sharper than all forty-three rivals combined. The Coordinator reached for **SEND** —

"*Wait.*"

A figure stepped out of the shadows in the corner, where he'd been the whole time, arms crossed, unimpressed. The **Critic**. The team's secret weapon and least popular member, whose entire job was to read the finished masterpiece and find the one flaw that would doom them all.

He read it cold. Top to bottom. Silence stretched. The deadline clock ticked into single digits.

"Page nine," he said finally. "One sentence promises something page four can't deliver. The Vorlani would've found it in three seconds, and it would've cost us everything." He tapped the line. "Fix that. *Now* it's ready."

Pricing groaned. Legal smiled. The Coordinator fixed the line.

And hit **SEND**.

---

The proposal — branded, beautiful, bulletproof — streaked up through the atmosphere and crossed the void to the waiting fleet. Eight billion people held one collective breath.

A long moment. Then every screen on Earth glowed gold again, with two words:

**BID ACCEPTED.**

The ships dipped — an actual little bow — and vanished into the dark, off to deliver the bad news to forty-three other planets.

In the quiet office, someone finally opened a window. Pricing high-fived Legal so hard it echoed. Technical Fit was already redesigning the celebration. Competitive was framing the rival bids as trophies. The Critic allowed himself exactly one half-smile before muttering, "It could've been tighter."

And the Coordinator just smiled, because she'd known it all along:

**Earth wasn't saved by one hero. It was saved by a swarm.** 🐝

*— The Swarmvengers will return.*
