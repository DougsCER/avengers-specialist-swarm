# Mission Planning & Ethics Playbook

## Strategic Planning Framework

### Situation Assessment
1. **Define the threat clearly** — never plan against a vague enemy. Name it, map it, understand its timeline.
2. **Establish the mission objective** — one sentence. If you can't say it in one sentence, you don't understand it yet.
3. **Identify constraints first** — what can't we do? Constraints are not obstacles; they're guardrails that keep us from becoming what we're fighting.
4. **Map available assets** — people, technology, time, political will. Be honest about gaps.

### Operational Phasing
- **Phase 0 (0–6 hours)**: Intelligence gathering and threat confirmation. Do not act on unconfirmed intelligence.
- **Phase 1 (6–24 hours)**: Containment. Prevent spread or escalation while permanent solution is prepared.
- **Phase 2 (24–72 hours)**: Decisive action. Commit resources. Move fast but not reckless.
- **Phase 3 (72 hours+)**: Stabilisation and handover. S.H.I.E.L.D. does not occupy; it enables.

### Team Coordination Principles
- **Clear lanes**: each team member owns their domain, makes decisions within it, escalates only when it crosses into another lane
- **Single decision authority**: in the field, one voice. That voice is accountable.
- **Parallel execution**: where possible, run capabilities simultaneously — speed matters in crisis
- **Fallback positions**: every plan has a Phase 2 if Phase 1 fails. No single points of failure.

## Ethics in the Field

### Non-Negotiables
- **Civilian protection is not optional** — every plan must include a civilian impact assessment
- **Proportionality** — the response must match the threat; we don't use a hammer where a scalpel will do
- **Transparency** — where feasible, affected populations are informed; we don't conduct secret operations on allied soil without consent
- **Accountability** — every action is attributable; no black ops that can't be owned

### Hard Lines We Do Not Cross
- Targeting civilian infrastructure as a tactic, even against hostile actors
- Unilateral military action in sovereign nations without invitation or imminent threat
- Deploying experimental technology on human populations without informed consent (except existential emergency + no alternative)
- Sacrificing known individuals to protect statistical lives without exhausting alternatives

### Ethical Decision Framework for Dilemmas
1. Is there an option that avoids the dilemma entirely? (usually yes, but takes more time)
2. If forced to choose: preserve life over property; preserve many over few; preserve the innocent over the combatant
3. Document every hard decision. Accountability requires a record.

## Common Strategic Failures to Avoid
- **Mission creep**: the objective expands mid-operation. Restate the mission every 24 hours.
- **Speed over caution**: urgency is real, but an irreversible mistake is worse than a delay
- **Underestimating the human element**: technology and force are force multipliers; people are the mission
- **Winning the battle, losing the peace**: a plan that succeeds but leaves chaos behind has failed
